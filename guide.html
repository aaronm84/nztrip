Field Guide

What goes in the pack, what goes on the plate, how the left side of the road works, and the five walks.

One mid-size case each, and it fits comfortably: wear the boots and shell on the plane, lean on merino, pack for six days not twelve — there's laundry in Wellington — and keep a quarter of the case empty for merino and wine coming home.

New Zealand is among the easiest countries on earth for this — likely easier than home. Coeliac awareness is high, menus label as standard practice, and "gluten free" is a regulated claim under NZ food law.

Why it works

  • Wellington, Queenstown and Auckland mark GF on menus as a matter of course.
  • Countdown, New World and Pak'nSave all carry real GF sections — stock up before the drive days.
  • The food culture leans our way: lamb, venison, seafood, vegetables. The wine needs no defence.

Flag at booking

  • The Hobbiton banquet — a fixed menu. The note goes on the reservation, not on the night.
  • The Milford cruise — likewise. Crews handle it beautifully with notice.
  • Say coeliac, not gluten free, when it must be treated as medical. The word lands differently there.

The thin stretches

  • National Park Village and Te Anau are small — carry provisions into both.
  • Aoraki Court rooms have full kitchens; one self-catered night takes all pressure off.
  • Every NZ booking form has a dietary field. Fill it in, every single time.

Most Americans are uneasy for two days and fine by the third. The left side isn't really the hard part — the narrow country roads are.

Where mistakes live

  • Pulling out of empty car parks with no traffic to copy. The charm to recite: driver stays toward the centerline.
  • Right turns become the big turn, across traffic.
  • Roundabouts run clockwise; yield to the right.
  • The wipers will fire instead of the indicator for three days. It happens to everyone.

Local knowledge

  • One-lane bridges are everywhere — the sign says who yields.
  • Add 20–30% to every drive time the map app promises.
  • Late autumn puts ice on shaded morning corners; drive the passes at midday.
  • An automatic, always — and confirm the car suits alpine roads.

The first 48 hours

  • First drive somewhere quiet, never straight from the airport into traffic.
  • The passenger's sworn duty on day one: call "stay left" at every single turn.
  • Keep the legs under 2.5 hours at the start. The mental load is real, then it's gone.

Five walks, none past four-ish hours, every one worth the whole day. The Great Walks season closes April 30 — but these day sections stay open, and stay spectacular.

2hEasy loop

Taranaki Falls · Tongariro

Across old lava flows out of Whakapapa, waterfall at the halfway mark, and Mt Ngauruhoe — Mt Doom — over your shoulder the entire way.

≤5hOut & back

Tama Lakes · Tongariro

Toward two crater lakes on open alpine tussock. Turn around whenever; the first lookout carries most of the reward. First to swap if early snow arrives.

3–4hGentle

Routeburn Flats · Mt Aspiring NP

The opening stretch of a Great Walk: beech forest, a river gorge, and snowline peaks at the turnaround meadow.

3hClimb

Key Summit · Fiordland

Off the Milford Road to a tarn-dotted plateau with mountains in every direction. The best reward-per-step on the whole trip.

3hFlat

Hooker Valley · Aoraki/Mt Cook

Three swing bridges and a finish at an iceberg-strewn glacier lake with Aoraki dead ahead. Famously the finest easy walk in the country.

The week before: check DOC track conditions — sections close for maintenance and avalanche control without much warning, and Hooker Valley has had partial closures recently.
