The Kit

Every tier works — the difference is fit-and-finish, weight, and how many years it lasts. The strategy: spend up where it touches weather and feet (shell, boots, the everyday base tops), save on the situational. And since cold is a feature of this trip, the kit deliberately under-insulates — the gold goes into staying dry and cutting wind.

Down & wet weather — the one rule. Down collapses when soaked, and Fiordland is the wettest ground we'll cross. The down jacket is for dry cold — mornings, evenings, town, the plane, worn under the shell. For actively wet hiking, a synthetic layer (Patagonia Nano Puff / Arc'teryx Atom, ~$239) still warms when damp — and given the cold tolerance, a grid fleece like the Patagonia R1 Air (~$139–159) will honestly be the most-worn layer of the trip.
The shell earns its keep — mist, wind, and sideways rain are the trip's default weather

Buying lore

Boots first, everything else waits

Buy boots by early 2027 and walk 30–50 miles in them before flying — the one item that punishes last-minute buying. Size up a half if between sizes; feet swell on descents. Everything else can wait for a sale.

REI is the one-stop cart

It stocks nearly every brand here, and member returns make boot-fit swaps painless. Watch the Anniversary Sale in May and REI Outlet for 20–30% off prior-season colors. Arc'teryx rarely discounts — buy when your size shows up.

Merino traps to dodge

Icebreaker Cool-Lite and Smartwool Sport are merino-poly blends; "Arms of Andes" is alpaca. Every pick on this page is confirmed 100% merino — the only unavoidable blends are gloves and socks, which need nylon to grip and survive.

Four pairs each — Fiordland is too wet for socks to dry overnight
Why merino runs the whole strategy: two long-sleeves, two tees, and bottoms per person is roughly five days of wear before the Wellington laundry stop — merino re-wears without complaint, which is the entire reason one mid-size checked bag each is enough. Add one bottle of Nikwax TX.Direct ($15) and re-treat both shells before flying; worn-off repellency is the number-one rain-jacket failure.
